Let me tell you about the first time I tried taking the CTA.

Imagine me: bright eyed, fresh out of high school, looking to start a new life in Chicago. I had always dreamed of living somewhere big; growing up in the middle of nowhere will do that to you. The thrill of independence kept me alert, excited.

Upon recieving my Ventra card, I meticulously planned my first trip: hop the Green line at IIT, ride two stops down to Roosevelt, walk half a mile to Target, and don't get attacked on the way there. I paced back and forth under the elevated tracks, heart pounding, head roaring with the echoes of the train rushing past me.

When I finally gathered the courage to scan my pass, I was denied. I tried to load fares onto the card, which also didn't work. Embarassed, I slinked back to my dorm, convinced I had done something wrong.

Turns out, you have to activate your card by phone. It's not like there's instructions on the back, or anything.

Since then, I've visited many different cities. Many transit systems nowadays encourage buying passes on your phone, but I much prefer collecting the physical cards. There's something uniquely exhilarating about recieving that card: in your hand, you have the opportunity to go anywhere. Catch the T, ride the subway, chase after an SF trolley: it's a new adventure. And I still fall in love with that independence - it's like I belong there. Or that I'm capable of something, so different than normal life.

As of 3/23/23, I've collected seven cards: Chicago, Toronto, Los Angeles, San Francisco, New York City, Boston, and Washington, DC. Since I am clearly an expert on all things public transit, I've decided to rank each of their systems by the following:

  1. Ease of Access: how easy is it to find their stations? Is navigation difficult? Are their elevators ever working? (2)
  2. Speed and Consistency: is taking the transit any faster than walking? And do the trains/buses ever ghost passengers? (3)
  3. User Experience (UX): how are the views? Are you comfortable, or always on edge because of the K9s swarming the car? (3)
  4. Aesthetic: how cool is the design of the stations? Will taking a picture be Instagram-able? (1)
  5. Card: how much does adding this card to my collection increase its value? (1)

Each category will be rated out of 10. The final scoring is achieved by multiplying each category score by its corresponding weight (as described above), then summed up. The maximum rating a city can achieve is 10/10. In mathematical notation:

Let's get started!

1. Chicago


Chicago boasts about its unique "L" trains, short for "elevated." Rather than running underground, most tracks lie overhead. It's second nature to go silent once a train passes above you, then continue your conversation afterwards. In total, there are eight lines, all of which converge in a "loop" shape in downtown Chi (thus, the inspiration behind the area's nickname: The Loop).

Fun fact: IIT has its own stop off the Green line, entitled "35th-Bronzeville-IIT." The track runs above our campus center, which was built second. To dampen the sound, the architect built a large metal structure enclosing the line, which makes for a very cool tunnel.

Ease of Access: For the most part, follow the tracks: since they're above ground, you'll find a stop eventually. Unfortunately, there's always two sets of stairs, only one of which you're allowed to go up. Additionally, very rarely will you see escalators at a station, let alone working escalators. The elevators aren't fantastic, either. Score: 4/10.

Speed and Consistency: Boasting a top speed of 55mph, train rides aren't nearly as bad. I can head to the Loop within 30 minutes by bus, or 15 minutes by train. On the other hand, consistency is hard to come by. Buses are quite unreliable, often passing by the stop or not showing up. There's been a handful of times when a line is out of service, so passengers must exit the train and take a shuttle bus to the next few stops, easily adding 20 minutes to your commute. Chicago, do better. Score: 3/10.

UX: I will say, taking the train is unpleasant if you're on half the lines (typicall those that run through the South Side). The Red line is notorious for being unsafe, especially at night, so I avoid it as much as I can. On the busiest days, you have to push your way in, or wait for the next train. The floors are usually pretty dirty, and salty in the winter. I won't comment on the smell. A huge pitfall of elevated tracks? Having to wait outside in winter months. There might be one or two heated areas, but they don't always work. The only reason this category gains any points: the views. It's beautiful, once you enter downtown. Passing between skyscrapers, looking over the Chicago river, gorgeous graffiti murals stretching 20 stories tall: there's nothing quite like it. Score: 3/10.

Aesthetic: Most of the time, it's not very pretty. The support beams are rusted, and the stations themselves aren't too inspiring. Nothing really to comment on. Score: 4/10.

Card: I have a special student pass with my picture on it, so not ideal. However, regular Ventra cards have a beautiful gradient of blue triangles, which is pleasing. Score: 7/10.

Calculated score: 3.7/10.
