In the last post, I discussed my week at JMM and my implicit sense of belonging in a world of mathematicians. This last weekend, I once again hopped on a plane (at an ungodly hour) to present my summer research at the Nebraska Conference for Undergraduate Women in Mathematics (NCUWM). Unlike JMM, this time it was solo: I was the only teammate presenting. Also unlike JMM: that belonging was more explicit.

Let me give you the stats. It was the 25th anniversary of NCUWM. About 230 students, invited speakers, and organizers attended. Of those, about 100 undergraduates presented research in a talk or poster. It was held Friday-Sunday, in the Embassy Suites in Lincoln, NE.

So, as you can imagine, it was a significantly smaller conference, and much more intimate.

The official theme this year was "Inspiring Women for Generations." Yet, with plenty of panels, plenaries, and breakout sessions, it was clear the conference sought to make mathematical spaces more inclusive and accessible overall. Invited speakers discussed mentoring, community, engaging the public, confidence gaps, opportunities, being a minority in mathematics, and of course, being a woman in mathematics.

I'll be honest. Despite being a female minority in a STEM field, I've never thought my race or gender had become detrimental to my work, or my experiences growing up. Maybe I'm one of the lucky ones: I always hear about micro-aggressions, or women having to earn respect they already deserved, and there's no doubt of blatant rasicm and sexism in professional spaces. Or maybe I'm not that observant: maybe I considered any hesitancy or self-doubt a result of my personality, rather than some intrinsic identity.

I've never felt out of place in a room full of male peers. I doubt I've actively noticed - maybe just passively. Call it overconfidence, or call it lack of awareness. In the end, when I listened to others share their frustrations and experiences as not just women, but as women mathematicians? It left me wondering: when, if at all, will I find myself in the same position? Am I too callous, or have the spaces I've been in always been inclusive?

For now, and especially at the conference, I'll just sit back and listen.

On the Inspirational

I'm not a huge fan of motivational talks, or discussions about "community" and "belonging". While finding people you connect with and care about is imperative, especially for incredibly social creatures like humans, an hour talk on why it's important begins to feel preachy. For me, there's a fine line between an inspirational spark and a didactic lecture.

I attended quite a few events which toed that line that weekend. Many of them were plenaries and panels, on discovering your mentors, advice, finding that community. That's not to insult the speakers: all of them were brilliant mathematicians, with a passion for advancing those spaces in math (and the work and experience to prove it). It just wasn't for me; however, I'm sure plenty felt seen, and found it empowering.

On Graduate School

At conferences, it's not a matter of whether you're going into academia or industry. It's a matter of which graduate school, pure math or applied math, research or teaching.

I'm not interested in academia. While I love math, I don't want it to be my entire life. I don't want to stress about quals, or the two body problem. The decision was easy; attending conferences in spite of that decision is not.

To illustrate: each talk I attended encouraged you to pursue grad school. It wasn't malicious, or pretentious; rather, they saw grad school as an opportunity many students failed to take out of fear. Nearly every invited speaker and guest held a PhD, or was a graduate student. Of the students I talked to, every one of them dreamed of going to grad school.
