In the last post, I discussed my week at JMM and my implicit sense of belonging in a world of mathematicians. This last weekend, I once again hopped on a plane (at an ungodly hour) to present my summer research at the Nebraska Conference for Undergraduate Women in Mathematics (NCUWM). Unlike JMM, this time it was solo: I was the only teammate presenting. Also unlike JMM: that belonging was more explicit.

Let me give you the stats. It was the 25th anniversary of NCUWM. About 230 students, invited speakers, and organizers attended. Of those, about 100 undergraduates presented research in a talk or poster. It was held for three days in the Embassy Suites in Lincoln, NE.

So, as you can imagine, it was a significantly smaller conference, and much more intimate.

The official theme this year was "Inspiring Women for Generations." Yet, with plenty of panels, plenaries, and breakout sessions, it was clear the conference sought to make mathematical spaces more inclusive and accessible overall. Invited speakers discussed mentoring, community, engaging the public, confidence gaps, opportunities, being a minority in mathematics, and of course, being a woman in mathematics.

I'll be honest. Despite being a female minority in a STEM field, I've never thought my race or gender had become detrimental to my work, or my experiences growing up. Maybe I'm one of the lucky ones: I always hear about micro-aggressions, or women having to earn respect they already deserved, and there's no doubt of blatant rasicm and sexism in professional spaces. Or maybe I'm not that observant: maybe I considered any hesitancy and self-doubt a result of my personality, rather than some intrinsic identity.

But ultimately, I've never felt out of place in a room full of male peers. I doubt I've actively noticed - maybe just passively. Call it overconfidence, or call it lack of awareness.
