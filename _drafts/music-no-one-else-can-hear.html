My relationship with music is complicated. The music seems to control me, rather than the other way around.

It wasn't always that way. Growing up, it was more detached. I certainly listened to songs: from my dad's record player, on the radio, watching music videos on YouTube. I made my first playlist in high school. It had no theme or meaning, as many tend to have. Instead, I just added songs I liked more than others, and would consistently only listen to that playlist. Throughout three years, I racked up 12 hours - ranging from Fall Out Boy and Panic! at the Disco to Maroon 5 and Taylor Swift.

Around that time, I became enamored with the lyrics. I would write lines on my arms in ink every day, and keep a list of which stanzas were my favorite. The criteria? How the words felt on my tongue. There were words that tasted sweet: swirling melodically, maybe a little heartbroken; words with a strong cadence: transitioning from slick to biting so fast, it left you breathless; words that echoed in your head: haunting and melancholic, crooning for a life you couldn't remember; words that danced in your mouth: popping and fizzing, like fireworks; words saturated with color, or

That interest - or maybe obsession - remains today, only with a different set of songs.

I branched out more once I reached my sophomore year of college. I let Spotify choose what I listened to, looping my "recommended" feed in the background while I worked on homework. When I started recognizing songs, and didn't hate them entirely, I'd add them to a new playlist. Many of them were indie, with little-known artists. They became familiar, much like my high school tracks.

Eventually, I started breaking ground in my Spotify library. I meticulously organized playlists into cascading folders and sub-lists, found the perfect names and captions, re-discovered old music, and stumbled upon new songs by chance. But, as I kept making playlists, I realized the they started entwining themselves into periods of my life.
