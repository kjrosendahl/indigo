My relationship with music is complicated. The music seems to control me, rather than the other way around.

It wasn't always that way. Growing up, it was more detached. I certainly listened to songs: from my dad's record player, on the radio, watching music videos on YouTube. I made my first playlist in high school. It had no theme or meaning, as many tend to have. Instead, I just added songs I liked more than others, and would consistently only listen to that playlist. Throughout three years, I racked up 12 hours - ranging from Fall Out Boy and Panic! at the Disco to Maroon 5 and One Direction.

Around that time, I became enamored with the lyrics. I would write lines on my arms in ink every day, and keep a list of which stanzas were my favorite. The criteria? How the words felt on my tongue.

There were words that tasted sweet: swirling melodically, maybe a little heartbroken; words with a strong cadence: transitioning from slick to biting so fast, it left you breathless; words that echoed in your head: haunting and melancholic, crooning for a life you couldn't remember; words that danced in your mouth: popping and fizzing, like fireworks; words saturated with color, or

That interest - or maybe obsession - remains today, only with a different set of songs.

I branched out more once I reached my sophomore year of college. I let Spotify choose what I listened to, looping my "recommended" feed in the background while I worked on homework. When I started recognizing songs, and didn't hate them entirely, I'd add them to a new playlist. Many of them were indie, with little-known artists. They became familiar, much like my high school tracks.

Eventually, I started breaking ground in my Spotify library. I meticulously organized playlists into cascading folders and sub-lists, found the perfect names and captions, re-discovered old music, and stumbled upon new songs by chance. But, that's when I realized: music had started to interweave into periods of my life.

There's an old scene from Mad Men, where Don Draper pitches Kodak's slide projector as part of an ad campaign. He flips through personal photos in the dim, smoky meeting room:

Likewise, when I listen to music, I'm swept backwards and forwards in time. It's more than a simple memory. All my senses are flooded: the same scents, images, emotions.

Sometimes, this is welcoming. Hearing "Young, Dumb, and Broke" takes me back to being buried in sand, half-asleep, feeling the sun's warmth, the taste of strawberries lingering in my mouth. "June" leaves me flushing, smelling the autumn breeze, adrenaline pumping. One of my favorite songs, "Maybe", slows down my heartrate, reminded of the way rain splatters across the windows of my campus center.

Other times, it renders me unable to listen to songs I once loved. "Tongue Tied" and "Girl" make me nauseous, while "Fall Together" hollows me out.

Selecting a certain playlist sets me back to the time I first made it. Some are inextricably tied to cities I've visited, or even to entire semesters. Music has become the fastest way to escape reality. A few times, I've created playlists right before traveling, with the hopes of binding the memories. It never worked. It felt inorganic, no matter how hard I tried.

So, where does that leave me?

When I feel lonely, I play Taylor Swift's Lover: it kept me company in San Fransisco. When I need motivation, I play Melanie Martinez's K-12: it's reminiscent of my runs along Lake Michigan. And when I ache to be somewhere else, or with someone else?

There's a song, waiting to be heard.
