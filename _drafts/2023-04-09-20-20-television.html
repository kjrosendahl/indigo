Rounding out the entertainment trifecta: my favorite TV shows. I've rewatched these multiple times, often late at night, freshly showered and curled up in bed. Shall we begin?

1. Sherlock (2010-2017)

Genre: mystery, crime, drama

Not to be a 2014 Tumblr girl, but Sherlock may be my favorite piece of media of all time. The world's most famous "consulting detective" is thrust into modern day London, solving murders and going toe-to-toe with his most infamous arch-nemeses. Of course, where would Sherlock Holmes be without his partner, Dr. John Watson, recently returned from Afghanistan and craving danger and adventure? Each time I watch an episode, I discover more and more hidden intricacies, interweaving into the most brilliant, clever, and satisfying storylines. This is heavily boistered by the charismatic performance of Benedict Cumberbatch (Benadryl Cucumber?) and his chemistry with Martin Freeman. From "A Study in Pink" to "The Final Problem": the game is on.

2. BoJack Horseman (2014-2020)

Genre: adult animation, black comedy, drama

Only this show can make you cry from lines uttered by an alcoholic horse. The eponymous character is a washed-up actor, addicted to pills, alcohol, and sex. His jaded and cynical tendencies strain every relationship he has, to the point of complete destruction. I both love and hate this show for its portrayal of mental illness and abuse, while still maintaining BoJack is not the hero of the story, no matter how many times he admits he is broken. At the same time, the comedic beats and witty, snappy dialogue of each episode nearly balances out the tragedy, strengthened by fantastic character-building and animation.

3. Demon Slayer: Kimetsu no Yaiba (2019-current)

Genre: anime, adventure, dark fantasy

My interest in anime was revived with this show. I didn't expect much: Tanjiro is a young teenager living in a world of ever-powerful demons. When his sister-turned-demon is the only survivor his family's slaughter, he trains as a Demon Slayer and vows to change his sister back. I'm not normally interested in fantasy, but the animation hooked me immediately. The demons are grotesque; each Slayer's fighting style is crisp, gorgeous, and a complete masterpiece to watch. Every arc, from Mugen Train to the Entertainment District, elevates the one before it, resulting in dazzling fight scenes and haunting screams. A nightmare worthy of binging, even if you don't like anime.

4. Mad Men (2007-2015)

Genre: period drama

Away from the action comes Mad Men, an slower-paced examination of American advertisement execs in the 1960s. Among the ensemble cast include: Don Draper, the enigmatic creative director; Peggy Olson, his secretary-turned-protégé; Pete Campbell, an ambitious young account exec; and Joan Holloway, the attractive office manager with more influence than meets the eye. The accuracy of old American culture, fashion, politics, power dynamics, social movements, and technology draws you into the decade, and missing one second means missing lovingly-added details that make scenes come alive. I'm not much for symbolism and cinematic interpretation, but I still enjoyed the every minute.

5. Kakegurui (2017-2019)

Genre: anime, psycological thriller, drama

The premise of Kakegurui sounds like your typical high school anime, except for one thing: each student's money, status, and even life is wagered in school-wide gambling. Enter Yumeko Jabami, a self-proclaimed "compulsive gambler" seeking to topple the student council president, who controls the school. The games and battles remind me of Sherlock and No Game, No Life: just when you think you know the answer, it shifts into something else entirely. Accompanying this is terrifying animation: quick-cuts turning cutesy school-girls into something a little more salacious, and a lot more disturbing. Watch it or not, but at least check out the coolest intro ever: "Deal with the Devil."

6. Fleabag (2016-2019)

Genre: black comedy, comedy-drama

It's hard describing this show without spoiling the plot, but! We follow a young woman in London, known only as Fleabag, as she struggles through loss, depression, and anger towards the world. Only a handful of characters are given names, but every one of them impacts her, for better or for worse. Part of this show's charm comes from its deft approach in breaking the fourth wall: you, the audience, become Fleabag's parter-in-confidence, exposed to her thoughts and experiences as if they were your own. Of course, this makes it all the more heartbreaking.

7. You (2018-current)

Genre: psychological thriller

I think everyone under the sun (and with access to Netflix) has seen this show. But, for those who haven't: Joe Goldberg is an obsessive psychopath who views his chosen women as something he needs to love and protect, at any cost. He considers himself an old-fashioned romantic, emulating the charismatic heroes of classic literature, all the while stalking and killing anyone who gets in the way. It's dangerous, and the popularity of Joe, much like BoJack, speaks to the common romanticization of protagonists, regardless of the monstrous things they do. I consider the first three seasons the best - the charm of old New York, warm haze of LA, and cold emptiness of suburbia set stage to a thrilling story both tightly written and scarily probable.
