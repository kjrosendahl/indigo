I was the type of kid who read at least one book a day.

I've always loved reading. Librarians knew me by name, and my parents always knew what to get me for Christmas. I stayed up way too late, just trying to finish the next chapter. In the car at night, I stole lines from passing streetlights.

Nowadays, I don't read as much as I want to. College takes up a lot of my time. But these books hold a special place in my heart: maybe they will for you, too.

1. A Little Life, Hanya Yanigahara

Genre: fiction, contemporary

Bookworm's Commentary: Running at 800+ pages, this visceral novel is more of a character study than anything else. It follows the life of Jude, a broken and enigmatic man haunted by his past, and his circle of friends in New York. Some view it as a masterpiece; others call it misery porn. I'm of the former opinion. It explores the nature of friendship, abuse, love, addiction, art, and what it means to be painfully human. It is the most beautiful book I've ever read, and also the most heartbreaking. Read at your own risk.

3. Speaker for the Dead, Orson Scott Card

Genre: science fiction

Bookworm's Commentary: I started this book as a sci-fi hater; in fact, the only reason I read it was because a friend recommended Ender's Game. Ender's Game was alright, but I heard the sequel was better. Indeed, it was: I poured through the pages, entranced by Ender's journey to Lusitania. The mystery of the 'piggies', the descolada virus, and a sentient super-powered AI capable of instantaneous communication across light-years? I've never been as hooked.

4. The Phantom Tollbooth, Norton Juster

Genre: children's fantasy

Bookworm's Commentary: This book serves as a love letter to the pursuit of knowledge. Whenever things get difficult, I turn to this book and rediscover the joy of learning. It stars Milo, a boy who "[regards] the process of seeking knowledge as the greatest waste of time," and his journey to rescue the princesses Rhyme and Reason. Clever and witty, this novel shines brighter the City of Illusions, sings sweeter than the Symphony of Color, and will definitely bring you out of the Doldrums.

5. American Gods, Neil Gaiman

Genre: fantasy

Bookworm's Commentary: Shadow Moon, having just served his prison sentence, re-enters the world and finds his wife dead and a stranger offering him a mysterious job. He is whisked across America, caught on the edge of a war between the Old Gods and the New Gods. As an avid traveler, but also as someone who grew up in the middle of nowhere, the pages hit deep, and hard. Gray winters in small towns, jukebox bars, gleaning cities, broken-down apartments in Chi, sea breeze in SF: Gaiman captured 21 years of my feelings in a single novel. It's bittersweet and deliciously dark.
